## 1. The Success Triangle (Ch2, 图2-1)

Falconi's core framework: every management challenge requires three elements working together.

```
        Leadership (领导力)
           /        \
          /          \
  Method (方法) —— Technical Knowledge (技术性知识)
```

- **Leadership**: Sets ambitious goals, holds people accountable, creates urgency, removes obstacles
- **Technical Knowledge**: Domain expertise — industry knowledge, process knowledge, technical skills
- **Method**: The structured approach — PDCA/SDCA, analysis tools, management systems

The triangle is multiplicative, not additive. If any element is zero, the result is zero:
- Method without leadership = bureaucracy (systems exist but no one drives them)
- Leadership without knowledge = recklessness (energy without direction)
- Knowledge without method = wasted potential (smart people, poor results)

## 2. The 13-Item Leadership System (Ch2, 图2-2)

Falconi defines a comprehensive system of what leaders must do. This is a structured daily/weekly
routine, not a personality trait list:

1. **Set and cascade goals** — Define targets for your area that connect to organizational goals
2. **Ensure goal decomposition** — Break your goals into sub-goals for the next level
3. **Guarantee analysis quality** — Don't accept superficial root cause analysis
4. **Approve action plans** — Review and approve plans created by your team
5. **Ensure resource allocation** — Provide people, budget, and tools for plan execution
6. **Track action plan execution** — Follow up weekly at minimum
7. **Remove obstacles** — When your team is blocked, clear the path
8. **Verify results (Check)** — Compare actual vs. planned at every review cycle
9. **Standardize successful practices** — When PDCA achieves results, transition to SDCA
10. **Develop people** — Build capability through training, coaching, and stretch assignments
11. **Promote knowledge acquisition** — Bring in external knowledge when internal isn't sufficient
12. **Build organizational culture** — Foster fact-based decision making, continuous improvement, collaboration
13. **Conduct daily management routine** — Use the 13-item daily work table systematically

### Leadership Accountability Chain
- CEO → organizational results
- Director → functional/departmental results
- Manager → process results
- Supervisor → operational results
- Accountability cannot be delegated upward or sideways

## 3. Leadership as the Engine

### What Sets Great Leaders Apart (Falconi's View)

Great leaders are not born — they are developed through practice. The key behaviors:

- **Obsessive about gaps**: They see every gap as personal. "That gap is MY problem."
- **Data-driven decisions**: They don't accept "I think" — they ask "show me the data"
- **Go to Gemba**: They verify at the source, not through reports alone
- **Develop successors**: A leader who can't be replaced can't be promoted
- **Never delegate verification**: Delegation of execution is fine; delegation of checking is abdication

### The Leader's Daily Rhythm
The daily work table (Chapter 9 details the full 13 items) creates a structured routine:
- Morning: Review indicators, check anomalies, address urgent issues
- During the day: Gemba walks, team check-ins, obstacle removal
- End of day: Follow up on delegated tasks, reflect, prepare for tomorrow

## 4. Knowledge Acquisition: Six Factors (Ch10)

Organizations are knowledge accumulation engines. Falconi identifies six factors that determine
how fast and how much knowledge an organization accumulates:

1. **Time (时间)** — Knowledge builds over time; learning curves are real. This means:
   - Hire for potential, not just current skill
   - Give people time to develop in roles
   - Don't expect instant results from new initiatives

2. **Goals (目标)** — Goals are THE engine of knowledge acquisition:
   - Ambitious goals create dissatisfaction with current knowledge
   - This dissatisfaction drives people to seek new knowledge
   - Easy goals = no learning. Impossible goals = despair. The sweet spot forces growth.

3. **Culture (文化)** — A culture of:
   - Continuous improvement and curiosity
   - Healthy dissatisfaction with the status quo
   - Respect for facts and data over hierarchy and opinion

4. **Motivation (动力)** — Intrinsic and extrinsic drivers (see Maslow section below)

5. **Standardization System (标准化体系)** — Standards are the documentation of practical knowledge:
   - Good daily management is the foundation of knowledge retention
   - Without standards, knowledge walks out the door with each departing employee

6. **Low Turnover (低流失率)** — People's minds hold tacit knowledge:
   - High turnover = massive knowledge loss
   - Retention strategies are knowledge strategies

## 5. Knowledge Creation Model (Ch10, 图10-1)

Four stages in the knowledge cycle, mapped to PDCA:

1. **Creation (创造)** — Point 1: Through analysis and synthesis during PDCA planning,
   new knowledge emerges from data and information
2. **Internalization (内化)** — Point 2: Knowledge is absorbed from external sources —
   technical literature, consultants, experts, courses, benchmarking visits
3. **Replication (复制)** — Point 3: Best practices are standardized and spread across the organization
4. **Transmission (传递)** — Point 4: Through training, certification, supervision, and on-the-job practice,
   knowledge flows to the people who need it

## 6. Company Knowledge Acquisition Phases (Ch10, 图10-4)

Organizations typically progress through three phases:

- **Phase 1: Internal knowledge mining** — Use existing internal knowledge (low-hanging fruit).
  Brainstorming sessions, cross-team sharing, best practice replication. Cheapest and fastest.

- **Phase 2: External knowledge import** — Bring in knowledge the organization doesn't have.
  Consultants, expert hires, training programs, benchmarking visits, conferences. Medium cost, medium time.

- **Phase 3: Original knowledge creation** — Create new knowledge through analysis, R&D, and experimentation.
  This is the hardest and most expensive phase, but yields the highest competitive advantage.
  Requires PhD-level analytical capability. (Toyota example: ~700 PhDs for problem solving.)

## 7. Maslow Applied to Organizations (Ch10)

Falconi applies Maslow's framework with two critical corrections to the common misunderstanding:

### Five Needs in Organizational Context

1. **Physiological (生理)** — Fair compensation, good working conditions, reasonable hours
2. **Safety (安全)** — Job security, safe workplace, social safety nets, predictable work environment
3. **Belonging (归属)** — Team collaboration, organizational identity, social connection, being part of something
4. **Esteem (尊重)** — Recognition, public acknowledgment, opportunities to demonstrate competence, respect
5. **Self-actualization (自我实现)** — Meaningful work, the joy of mastery, passion for what you do, growth

### Two Critical Properties (Often Misunderstood)

**Simultaneity (同时性)**: All five needs are active simultaneously, not sequentially.
A person doesn't stop needing safety just because they have food. Leaders must address
ALL levels at the same time, not treat them as sequential stages.

**Collectivity (集体性)**: These needs must be addressed for groups, not just individuals.
This means preventive, systemic approaches (good HR systems, fair compensation structures,
team-building practices) rather than reactive, individual interventions.

### Psychological Potential (心理潜能)
- Every person has a maximum learning capacity (the "potential curve")
- Actual knowledge acquired is usually far below potential (the "reality curve")
- The gap = "learning loss" — wasted human potential
- Companies must create environments that challenge people toward their potential
- Goal-setting is the primary mechanism: goals difficult but achievable through effort

## 8. The Learning Pipeline Model (Ch10, 图10-3)

Knowledge flows from sources through a "pipeline" into the person:

```
[Goal/Need] → [Knowledge Source] → [Psychological Potential] → [Motivation Valve] → [Knowledge Acquired]
   Point 1         Point 2              Point 3                  Point 4
```

- **Point 1 — Trigger**: Goals or new roles create dissatisfaction with current knowledge level
- **Point 2 — Source**: Available knowledge sources (courses, consultants, books, colleagues, data)
- **Point 3 — Capacity**: Psychological potential determines the maximum flow speed
- **Point 4 — Valve**: Motivation is the valve — if closed, no knowledge flows regardless of potential

Implication for managers: you must work on ALL four points:
1. Set goals that create healthy dissatisfaction (not too easy, not impossible)
2. Provide access to knowledge sources
3. Hire and develop for potential
4. Create a motivating environment (address all five Maslow needs)

## 9. Knowledge Management Strategies (Ch10)

### Two Strategies (Hansen et al.)

**Codification (编纂策略)**
- Extract knowledge into documents, databases, software, manuals, SOPs
- Best for: standardized products, explicit knowledge, replicable processes
- The knowledge exists independent of the person
- 80/20 split favoring codification for standardized businesses

**Personalization (个性化策略)**
- Knowledge transfers person-to-person through mentoring, collaboration, dialogue
- Best for: customized products, tacit knowledge, creative work
- The knowledge lives in people's heads
- 80/20 split favoring personalization for customized businesses

### Three Knowledge Sources
1. **Analysis and synthesis** during PDCA planning (learning by doing)
2. **Internal and external technical knowledge** (importing proven solutions)
3. **Best practice replication** (spreading what already works)
