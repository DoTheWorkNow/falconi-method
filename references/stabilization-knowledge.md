## 1. Why Stabilization Matters

Falconi uses the human body analogy: a healthy body runs itself — you don't consciously
control your heartbeat or digestion. A well-managed company should work the same way.
Daily management creates this "self-running" capability.

**The critical rule: SDCA before PDCA.**

You cannot improve a process that isn't stable. If results fluctuate wildly, you first need
to standardize (SDCA) before attempting improvement (PDCA). Otherwise you're building on quicksand.

## 2. The SDCA Cycle

**S — Standardize**: Define the correct way to perform the work (Standard Operating Procedures)
**D — Do**: Train people and execute according to the standard
**C — Check**: Verify that standards are being followed and results are stable
**A — Act**: If an anomaly occurs, investigate and either correct the deviation or update the standard

### When to Use SDCA
- After a PDCA improvement achieves its target (lock in the gains)
- When processes show high variation (stabilize before improving)
- When anomalies (deviations from standard) occur
- As the foundation of daily management

## 3. Daily Management: The Five Steps

Step 1: **Set specifications** — Define what "good" looks like for every output
- Quality specifications, delivery targets, cost parameters
- Must be measurable and observable

Step 2: **Set standards** — Define HOW to achieve the specifications
- Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)
- Process parameters, checkpoints, decision rules

Step 3: **Train** — Ensure every person can execute the standard
- Operational training (how to do the task)
- On-the-job training (practice under supervision)
- Certification (verify competence)

Step 4: **Verify** — Check that standards are followed and specifications are met
- Regular audits and inspections
- Statistical monitoring of process outputs
- Supervisory observation

Step 5: **Correct deviations** — When anomalies occur, act immediately
- Contain the immediate problem
- Investigate root cause
- Update standard if needed
- Retrain if needed

## 4. Standardization System

### What Gets Standardized
- Critical processes that affect quality, cost, delivery, or safety
- Work methods that have been proven effective
- Decision rules for common situations
- Measurement and inspection methods

### Quality Assurance (ISO 9001 alignment)
A complete standardization system is the sedimentation of practical knowledge.
Standards are the documentation of explicit knowledge. They should be:
- Clear enough for any trained person to follow
- Specific enough to produce consistent results
- Updated regularly based on new knowledge

### The Paradox of Standards
Standards are not constraints — they are the foundation for improvement.
Without standards, every person invents their own method, and you can't identify
what works best. Standards capture current best practice and make improvement possible
by giving you a baseline to measure against.

## 5. Daily Management Diagnosis

### The 30-Item Evaluation (Table 9-1)

Falconi provides a comprehensive diagnostic covering PDCA and SDCA activities.
Key areas evaluated:

**SDCA items:**
- Are standards documented and accessible?
- Are people trained on the standards?
- Is compliance verified regularly?
- Are anomalies detected and addressed promptly?
- Is the root cause of anomalies investigated?

**PDCA items:**
- Are improvement targets set based on gaps?
- Is analysis rigorous (data-based, not opinion)?
- Are action plans specific (who, what, when)?
- Is execution tracked?
- Are results verified against targets?

### Three-Level Evaluation System (Figure 9-2)
1. **Self-assessment** by the team
2. **Manager assessment** by the direct supervisor
3. **Director assessment** by senior leadership

Each level checks not just results but the PROCESS of management.

### Management Development Matrix (Figure 9-3)
Plot teams/departments on two axes:
- X-axis: Daily management diagnosis score (maturity of management process)
- Y-axis: Goal achievement rate (results)

Four quadrants:
- **High process + High results** = Excellent (sustain and share best practices)
- **High process + Low results** = Good process, need better goals or knowledge
- **Low process + High results** = Lucky or hero-dependent (fragile, will regress)
- **Low process + Low results** = Priority for management development

## 6. Knowledge Acquisition in Organizations

### The Core Insight
Organizations are knowledge accumulation engines. The more knowledge accumulated
and the better it's standardized and replicated, the better the results.

### Six Factors for Knowledge Accumulation
1. **Time** — Knowledge builds over time; learning curves are real. Hire for potential.
2. **Goals** — Goals are the engine of knowledge acquisition. Ambitious goals force learning.
3. **Culture** — A culture of continuous improvement, curiosity, and dissatisfaction with status quo.
4. **Motivation** — HR strategies that develop people's psychological well-being.
5. **Standardization system** — Standards are the documentation of practical knowledge. Good daily management is essential.
6. **Low turnover** — People's minds hold tacit knowledge. High turnover = knowledge loss.

### Knowledge Creation Model (Figure 10-1)
Four stages in the PDCA framework:
1. **Creation** (Point 1): Through analysis, new knowledge emerges from data and information
2. **Internalization** (Point 2): Knowledge is absorbed from technical sources, consultants, experts, books
3. **Replication** (Point 3): Best practices are standardized and spread
4. **Transmission** (Point 4): Through training, certification, supervision, and on-the-job practice

### Company Knowledge Acquisition Phases (Figure 10-4)
- **Phase 1**: Use existing internal knowledge (low-hanging fruit, brainstorming)
- **Phase 2**: Bring in external knowledge (consultants, experts, training, benchmarking)
- **Phase 3**: Create new knowledge through analysis, R&D, and experimentation (hardest, highest payoff)

## 7. The Learning Organization Model

### Maslow's Two Concepts Applied to Organizations

**Psychological Potential (心理潜能)**
- Every person has a maximum learning capacity (the "potential curve")
- Actual knowledge acquired is usually far below potential (the "reality curve")
- The gap = "learning loss" — wasted human potential
- Companies must create environments that challenge people to reach their potential
- Goal-setting is the primary mechanism: goals that are difficult but achievable through effort

**Motivation (动力) = Psychological Health**
Maslow's five needs, applied to organizational management:
1. **Physiological** — Fair compensation, good working conditions
2. **Safety** — Job security, safe workplace, social safety nets
3. **Belonging** — Team collaboration, organizational identity, social connection
4. **Esteem** — Recognition, public acknowledgment, opportunities to demonstrate competence
5. **Self-actualization** — Meaningful work, the joy of mastery, passion for what you do

**Two critical properties of these needs:**
- **Simultaneity** — All five needs are active simultaneously (not sequential as commonly misunderstood)
- **Collectivity** — These needs must be addressed for groups, not just individuals (preventive approach)

### The Learning Pipeline Model (Figure 10-3)
Knowledge flows from sources through a "pipeline" into the person:
- **Trigger** (Point 1): Goals or new roles create dissatisfaction with current knowledge
- **Source** (Point 2): Available knowledge sources (courses, consultants, books, colleagues)
- **Capacity** (Point 3): Psychological potential determines flow speed
- **Valve** (Point 4): Motivation is the valve — if closed, no knowledge flows regardless of potential

## 8. Knowledge Management

### Two Strategies (Hansen et al.)

**Codification (编纂)**
- Extract knowledge into documents, databases, software, manuals
- Best for: standardized products, explicit knowledge, replicable processes
- The knowledge exists independent of the person

**Personalization (个性化)**
- Knowledge transfers person-to-person through mentoring, collaboration, dialogue
- Best for: customized products, tacit knowledge, creative work
- The knowledge lives in people's heads

### Choosing Your Strategy
- Standardized business + explicit knowledge → Codification (80/20 split)
- Customized business + tacit knowledge → Personalization (80/20 split)
- Most companies need both, with one dominant

### Knowledge Sources
1. Analysis and synthesis during planning (learn by doing PDCA)
2. Internal and external technical knowledge
3. Best practice replication

## 9. Falconi's 17 Concluding Principles

1. Management = pursuing better results through continuous improvement
2. Focus on stakeholder satisfaction, especially financial performance
3. Functional management is the best way to serve stakeholders
4. Develop systems thinking — understand structure, process, function
5. Three organizational levels: organization, process, operation — each needs goals, design, management
6. Don't neglect redesigning each level continuously
7. Leadership requires knowledge. Build your "leadership content"
8. Knowledge only comes through understanding. Master the art of asking good questions
9. Three knowledge sources: best practices, information/data, and people
10. Better daily management → better knowledge absorption
11. Knowledge in data can only be extracted through systematic analysis and synthesis
12. Databases and IT are transforming management — use them
13. Stay open to external knowledge: courses, books, conferences, consultants
14. Your team's motivation determines your ceiling. Put the right people in the right places
15. Never assume action plans will execute themselves — verify
16. Models are essential for analysis, synthesis, learning, and communication
17. Your organization is a school. The better the school, the better the results
