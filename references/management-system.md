## 1. The Management System Model

Falconi's management system has three interconnected layers:

```
Strategic Management (5 years)
    ↓ Long-term direction, vision, major bets
Policy Management (1 year)
    ↓ Annual breakthrough goals, cross-functional projects
Daily Management (ongoing)
    ↓ Standardization, routine operations, anomaly handling
```

Strategic Management sets direction. Policy Management translates it into annual targets.
Daily Management maintains and incrementally improves the operational base.

The common mistake: organizations focus on strategy and neglect daily management.
Result: brilliant plans with terrible execution.

## 2. Strategic Management (5-Year Horizon)

### Purpose
Set the long-term direction that will satisfy all four stakeholders:
shareholders, customers, employees, and society.

### Key Activities
1. Define the organization's ideal state (5 years out)
2. Identify the major gaps between current and ideal
3. Set strategic priorities
4. Allocate resources to close the most critical gaps

### Important Principle
Strategy without goals is philosophy. Goals without strategy is hope.
The two must connect: strategy defines WHERE to go; goals define HOW FAR and BY WHEN.

## 3. Policy Management (Annual Goals / 方针管理)

### Purpose
Translate strategic priorities into concrete annual breakthrough goals that cascade
through the organization.

### The Cascade Process
1. **CEO/Top management** sets 3-5 annual breakthrough goals (not more)
2. Each goal is decomposed into sub-goals for the next level
3. Sub-goals are further decomposed until they reach actionable operational targets
4. Each level negotiates targets with the level above (catchball process)

### Key Rules
- Breakthrough goals only — don't put routine operations in policy management
- Each goal must have: a measurable target, a deadline, and an owner
- Goals cascade vertically (through hierarchy) AND horizontally (across functions)
- The number of breakthrough goals should be small to maintain focus

## 4. Goal Decomposition

### From Strategy to Operations

```
Strategic indicators (CEO level)
  → General indicators (Director level)
    → Operational indicators (Manager/Supervisor level)
      → Task-level standards (Worker level)
```

### Gap → Target Conversion
- Gap = Ideal performance - Current performance
- Target ≈ 50% of gap (ambitious but achievable)
- Target must be technically grounded (not arbitrary)

### Decomposition Rules
1. Decompose by function first, then by department
2. Each sub-goal must mathematically or logically roll up to the parent goal
3. The person who owns the goal must participate in setting it
4. Every indicator at every level must have: current value, target value, deadline

## 5. Functional vs. Department Management

This is one of Falconi's most important distinctions:

### Functional Management (横向/跨部门)
- Organized around OUTCOMES (quality, cost, delivery, safety, morale)
- Crosses departmental boundaries
- Led by a "functional owner" (often a senior director)
- Focuses on end-to-end processes that deliver stakeholder value
- This is how excellent companies are actually managed

### Department Management (纵向/部门内)
- Organized around organizational units
- Stays within departmental boundaries
- Led by department heads
- Focuses on daily operations within the department

### The Critical Insight
Most companies are managed only vertically (by department). But value flows
HORIZONTALLY through processes that cross departments. If you only manage vertically,
the gaps between departments become black holes where quality, cost, and delivery
problems fester.

Functional management is harder to implement but it's what separates good companies
from great ones. It requires cross-functional authority and a culture of collaboration.

## 6. Leadership System

### Leadership as the Engine
In Falconi's triangle (Leadership × Knowledge × Method), leadership is the human engine
that drives everything. Without strong leadership, knowledge and method are inert.

### What Leaders Must Do
1. **Set ambitious goals** — Goals that force the organization to grow
2. **Ensure analysis is rigorous** — Don't accept superficial root cause analysis
3. **Guarantee execution** — Remove obstacles, follow up, hold accountable
4. **Verify results** — Check personally, don't just read reports
5. **Develop people** — Build capability at every level
6. **Build culture** — Focus on results, continuous improvement, fact-based decisions

### Leadership Accountability Chain
- The CEO is accountable for organizational results
- Each director is accountable for their functional/departmental results
- Each manager is accountable for their process results
- Each supervisor is accountable for operational results
- Accountability cannot be delegated upward or sideways

## 7. The 13-Item Daily Work Table

Falconi prescribes a structured daily routine for leaders. The 13 items cover:

1. Review priority indicators (are we on track?)
2. Check anomaly reports from the previous day
3. Address unresolved anomalies
4. Review action plan progress
5. Conduct gemba walks (go see the actual work)
6. Hold brief team check-ins
7. Review customer feedback/complaints
8. Address people issues (attendance, training, morale)
9. Review safety incidents
10. Follow up on delegated tasks
11. Prepare for upcoming reviews/presentations
12. Invest in personal development/learning
13. Reflect on what worked and what didn't

The daily work table is the operational rhythm of management. It ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

## 8. Organizational Performance (Rummler's Framework)

### The 3×3 Matrix

Rummler identified that organizational performance is determined by 9 variables:
three levels of performance × three needs at each level.

**Three Levels:**
1. **Organization level** — The enterprise as a whole and its relationship with the market
2. **Process level** — The cross-functional workflows that produce outputs
3. **Operation level** — Individual jobs and tasks

**Three Needs at Each Level:**
1. **Goals** — Are clear performance objectives set?
2. **Design** — Is the structure/process/job designed to achieve the goals?
3. **Management** — Is performance actively managed and improved?

### How to Use This Framework

For any organizational problem, diagnose across all 9 cells:

| Level | Goals | Design | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Organization** | Strategic goals aligned with stakeholders? | Org structure supports strategy? | Company performance tracked & acted on? |
| **Process** | Process targets linked to org goals? | Processes designed efficiently? | Process performance monitored? |
| **Operation** | Task standards clear & measurable? | Jobs designed for success? | Individual performance managed? |

Most organizations have some cells well-addressed and others neglected.
The neglected cells are where performance leaks occur.

### Common Patterns
- "Strategy is clear but execution is poor" → Check Process Design and Operation Management
- "People are working hard but results don't improve" → Check Process Design and Organization Design
- "We have good processes but inconsistent results" → Check Operation Management and Process Management
